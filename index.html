apps related to medication. Next, I took the responsibility of finding quantitative-based insights of how many medication users in the U.S. use phone apps to track

With the statistics provided above, we were set in wanting to create an app solely on improving Medication-taking and tracking. Rather than trying to create an entirely

Finding Data: Quantitative and Qualitative Measures
Our Survey Findings

Our purpose in creating our survey was to quantifiably measure how certain U.S. individuals from the age range of 30+ take and track their medications. Our survey was divided into 2 portions: medication taking habits and how people use their phones.

Results From First Portion Results From Second Portion

From our survey findings, there was an estimate of 26.9% of our survey takers who have reported take at least 1 medication. Additionally, 53.8% of our survey takers reported having their phone every day is a priority.

Our Interview Findings

The type of people we had interviewed were in the age demographic of 40-60 and surprisingly, we found that people don’t have any problems using their phones.
Rather than trying to implement new ways of making medication-tracking, we decided to focus on what people like about their phones in hopes of implementing
our findings in our mobile app design.

Data Synthesis

From our findings, we found that people were able to take their medications based on habits. Upon iterating, our main goal for creating our app was to think of ways making medication-taking natural and easy.

Conducting Our Competitor Analysis

With wanting to create an app that can be used incorporated in anyone’s daily lives, we had to figure out exactly how leading apps succeeded. We as a team conducted a cross-comparative competitor analysis to to gather insights about what we liked and what can be improved.

Recurring Themes From Our Analysis
  • 1. Both Medication Taking Apps on Android and Apple Have Simple Navigation
  • 2. Users couldn't keep track of their health-related needs in one app.

From the data we collected, we now had our goals to make medication-tracking much easier and simpler to use through the process of easy navigation and having everything
in one place. Now it’s just the matter of creating our potential user: an every-day guy who wants to live a healthy life and do what he loves every single day.

Our user Alfred is already a tech-savvy guy and one who knows how to use his phone. But keeping both him, and the data we received on how people used their phones, we didn’t want to assume that everyone has an easy means of navigating. So, our next task was to figure out: What are the steps users, like Alfred, would take in terms of fulfilling their goal?… But first, let’s establish our tasks.

Our Tasks For Alfred
  1. To create a medication reminder for Lisinopril.
  2. To create a reminder to check Blood Pressure (BP).
  3. To confirm you've taken Lisinopril in 7 hours.

Keeping our Tasks in Mind, it was now time to create our User Flow.

As you can see from the “Choose Type of Reminder” action, our steps for creating certain reminders are meticulous and in a series of “one-by-one”. Habits can’t be learned overnight nor can they fully be accepted overnight either. So, the best way to create a habit is to build. “As you build, break habits into chunks” (James Clear).
For our Blood Pressure reminder specifically, we wanted our users to be able to manually put in their respective info (Systolic, Diastolic, and BPM) so they have control over the information they have. Additionally, by inputting results every time, our goal was to make sure that our users also know what goes in to checking their Blood Pressure through constantly doing it over and over again.
Finally, constructing our flows and our certain tasks for the current motivation, it’s now time to present our first current wireframes!

Lo-Fi Wireframing Before (A/B/C Testing)

Presented here are the wireframes for creating a reminder for Lisinopril. For my initial wireframes, I really wanted this step-by-step process to be done in a top down and linear style. I wanted the user to sequentially fill out all of the information before moving onto the very second page. I believe that our user shouldn’t forget about anything regarding their health and they should have the feeling that their health can be prioritized. But, with this being my very first case study, it’s important to showcase what struggles I came about and the things I think about now as opposed to when I was creating these wireframes.

Here are some quick iterations I did just now. I was not the same student I was 3 months ago but efforts in critically thinking about layout and structure are paramount for a great experience. As for my design wireframes, I really needed to improve my efforts in consistency: consistency through the buttons and through the spacing. Additionally, one thing I overlooked was a back button! What if my User wanted to go back?? How could they? I knew at this point, I had strengths in research, but I needed more practice in UX Designing. Throughout my portfolio, I will be showcasing how I did just that but this particular project, and thankfully with collaboration and feedback, really emphasized the importance of creating an effective design!

A/B/C Wireframe Testing

Recurring "I Likes..."

  • 1. Our testers liked the ability to see a dropdown menu.
  • 2. They liked the interactive ways of customizing their health.
  • 3. People liked the sequential way of making their reminders.

Recurring "I Wishes..."

  • 1. Our Testers wished for more clarity in the “+” and dropdown buttons.
  • 2. They wanted to see more content on the pages related to their reminders.
  • 3. They wished for more consistency and clarity in the buttons.
